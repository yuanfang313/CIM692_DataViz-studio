A World on the Cover

An overview of Sanlian Lifeweek from 2003 - 2018

by Yuan Fang

Sanlian Lifeweek is a magazine in China, which was first founded in 1925 (known as Life weekly then). In 1926, Zou Taofen (邹韬奋) became editor of the magazine and “changed its mission from vocational education to political reporting and social criticism”. The magazine increased in circulation under Zou’s editorship from 2,800 in 1925 to 80,000 in 1930. In 1932, it created the highest circulation record in China, which issued 150,000 copies per issue. In 1933, it was closed by the government.



Now, Sanlian lifeweek is one of the most respectful media in China. So, I decided to do some analysis of the headlines on the covers of Sanlian Lifeweek from 2003 – 2018 (the time range that’s covered by its archive online), to see how its contents had changed over time.


The last issue of April from 2003 to 2018


The method to do the analysis is quite simple, which is to classify all the headlines on the covers into different categories and subcategories.

The Trend of Main Categories (2003 - 2018)

After recording almost all the headlines on its covers from 2003 to 2018, the first layer of categories of headlines on Sanlian Lifeweek finally were confirmed. For the number of the headlines in each year is different, the values in a chart below is calculated as the proportion of a particular category in a year.

As you can see below, the increasing patterns appear in the categories: Culture, Education, Science and Technology, Personal Issue and Cultivation; the decreasing patterns appear in the categories: Economy,Politics and News Events.

The Trend of Subcategories (2003 - 2018)


Here are the detailed contents about subcategories of headlines. Except the “NA” category, I selected the 6 subcategories that have the maximum proportions of each category to look into the shape of data, of which the quantitative relation is shown by the bar charts beneath.

Hover over each bar in the bar charts to see how the proportion of a subcategory had changed every four years from 2013 to 2018.

Culture

Generally, “Culture” has larger proportion than other first categories along the timeline. It’s consisted of 10 subcategories. Except the 6 subcategories that have the maximum proportions, the rest categories are: Literature, History, Video Games, Design, Religion.

Other Findings:

1. Foreign Culture, Literature, Video Games, Design, Religion hadn’t appeared until 2007.

2. Only one headline belongs to Religion, which appeared in 2010. It is about the discussion of “the new meanings of Buddhism in modern society”.

Economy

Generally, the proportion of “Economy” reduced from 2003 to 2018. It had a seemingly rapid growth period from 2006 to 2008 (the year of Beijing Olympic Games). The category, “Economy” is consisted of 11 subcategories, the rest of which are: Trade, Pricing, Foreign Economy, Employment, Consumer Spending, New Economy.

Other Findings:

1. The contents in the subcategory, “Foreign Economy” are all about Asian counties, which are Vietnam (2008), Dubai (2010), Iran (2012), Japan (2012), North Korea (2017), and Saudi Arabia (2018).

2. It is not until 2015, “New Economy” appeared. There are only two headlines falling into this subcategory, which are Sharing Economy (2015), Knowledge Economy (2018).

Politics

The category, “Politics” is consisted of 11 subcategories, the rest of which are: Judicial System, Military, Infrastructure, Diplomacy, Political System.

Other Findings:

1. There are 8 headlines falling into “Warfare”, 7 of which are about Iraq War (5/7) and Gulf War (2/7). And they all appeared in 2003.

2. There are 5 headlines falling into “Military”, which only focus on two kinds of contents: Navy (3/5) and Military Parade (2/5).

3. Nearly half of the headlines (8/19) falling into “Foreign Politics” are about Countries in Middle East.

4. Most headlines (6/8) falling into “International Politics” are about territorial disputes.

Science and Technology

The category, “Science and Technology” is consisted of 8 subcategories, the rest of which are: Pharmaceutical Technology and Physics.

Other findings: From 2003 to 2018, there were 6 headlines falling into “Computer Science”, which only focused on two topics: Artificial Intelligence (4/6) and Virtual Reality (2/6).

Social Issues

The category, “Social Issues” is consisted of 8 subcategories, the rest of which are: Science and Ethical Issues and Disaster.

Figure(individuals)

The category, “Figure” refers to people who were introduced as individuals who were not criminals. This category is consisted of 14 subcategories, the rest of which are: Military Commanders, Scientists, Athletes, Astronauts, Doctors, Philosophers, Professors and Translators.

The individuals whose occupations only appeared once from 2003 to 2018 are classified as “NA”, which consist 8.9% of this category.

Other Findings: From 2003 to 2018, there were 4 headlines falling into “Scientist”, which only focused on: Mathematicians (2/4) and Physicists (2/4).

For “Figure” who were narrated as individual but were criminals, its quantitive relation with “Figure” who were not criminals are shown as beneath:

The total number of "criminals" in the headlines from 2003 to 2018 is 27, in which “Politicians” (7/27) and “Entrepreneurs & Businessmen ” (5/27) were the most.

Figures(groups)

The category, “Figures” refers to people who were introduced as groups. This category has 35 records in total which is consisted of 7 subcategories.

The group of people whose occupations or identities only appeared once from 2003 to 2018 are classified as “NA”, which consist 40% of this category. For instance, Gourmets, Network mobs, Outdoor enthusiasts, Politicians, Farmers, Doctors, Teachers, Couriers and so on.

Other Findings:

1. There are 3 headlines falling into “Patients”, which were about patients of AIDS (2/3) and hemophiliac(1/3).

2. No criminals were narrated as groups in the "Figures"

A world on the cover

When I started to think about my perspective of this project, I asked myself a wired question: what would it be like if a person just lives in “a world on the cover” ? The answer might be: the person will never know he or she lives “on the cover”, and will never fully know about this world. Somehow, it is also true for ourselves.

Although “the world” on the cover of Sanlian Lifeweek is just a tiny fragment of the reflection of our “real world”, one way to approach our own reality might be to find those “fragments”, and piece them together.
